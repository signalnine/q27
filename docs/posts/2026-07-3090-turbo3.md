# Your 24GB 3090 can serve Claude Code at 131K context

*(draft. Engine: Quasar/q27 -- github.com/signalnine/q27, models at
huggingface.co/signalnine/Qwen3.6-27B-MTP-q27)*

A 3090 running Qwen3.6-27B has historically been a 32K-context box. The
first benchmark run proved it the direct way: three sessions, three
deaths on the context wall at turn ten. That cap was never compute --
it was KV bytes. This post is about a 3-bit KV cache that turns the
same card into a 131K box serving Claude Code at 102 tokens/sec median,
and the measurement that made it shippable when the quant's own
reference implementation refuses to try.

## turbo3, in one paragraph

QuaRot-family KV quantization: L2-normalize each 128-dim group, rotate
through a baked Walsh-Hadamard transform, 3-bit quantize against 8
Lloyd-Max centroids with a corrected per-block norm. 50 bytes per 128
dims = 13.4 KB per token, against fp8's 34 and fp16's 68. The
correctness contract is the pretty part: dequantized K equals rotated
K, the rotation is orthonormal, so rotating Q once after rope leaves
every attention score unchanged; V pools in the rotated basis and one
inverse transform after the combine un-rotates everything. Three small
kernels. The softmax never notices.

I wrote the original as a llama.cpp fork patch; this is the same
format ported into my own engine, microtest-first, bit-for-bit against
the fork's CPU reference before any engine wiring.

## The guard that measurement retired

My own fork refuses 3-bit K when the GQA ratio is >= 6 -- an early 7:1
model cratered to perplexity 2887 and the guard got written wide. This
model sits at exactly 6.0, so the fork silently upgrades K to 8-bit
and pays for it in bytes. Instead of trusting my own guard I measured
it: teacher-forced NLL, four KV configs, one binary.

    fp16 7.317 | fp8 7.327 | turbo3 V-only 7.368 | turbo3 K+V 7.381

Three-bit V costs +0.70%. Adding 3-bit K costs another +0.17%. The
crater does not exist here -- at head_dim 256 the rotation runs over
two independent 128-groups per head, each with its own norm.

Independent confirmation, from the other side: the maintainer of the
fork I took the quant from forced his own build's guard off
(`TURBO_AUTO_ASYMMETRIC=0`) and got +0.87% total on the same model,
citing the same head_dim-256 two-group mechanism -- without having seen
my triage. Two engines, one finding. If you only trust a
single-engine perplexity number from the person shipping the engine,
here it is reproduced on a codebase I did not write. Depth
validation: position-bucketed NLL flat to 297K, needle retrieval 6/6
exact on a 361,513-token prompt (two needles beyond the native
window), and speculative acceptance ties fp8 to the third decimal on
matched replay. The over-wide guard costs the fork exactly the
98K-to-131K context gap on this card.

## The decode flip

Day one of 3090 serving, llama.cpp decoded 15% faster than me -- my
verify attention ran a register-accumulator fallback because Ampere
has no fp8 tensor cores. Profile first: verify was 53% of the decode
round. The fix was an fp16-MMA verify kernel built from the e4m3
kernel's geometry and the prefill kernel's fragment idioms -- simpler
than both donors, and it lands turbo3, fp8, and fp16 KV as half tiles
on any sm_80+ card. Result: +32% decode on matched replay, and the
live benchmark flipped from -13% to **+27% over llama's best same-card
config** (102.2 vs 85.6 t/s median), at 131K context vs llama's 82K.

Then the profile ended the story cleanly: the GEMV family runs at
81-90% of DRAM speed-of-light on this card. Two planned follow-ups
(cp.async staging, a width-10 build) both measured negative and died
in an afternoon. The 3090 is now parked at its weight roofline, which
is where a bandwidth-bound engine should be parked.

## The honest Ampere ledger

Decode: q27 +19% over mainline llama.cpp (+27% over the tuned fork
leg). Context ceiling: q27 131K vs 82-98K, +34-60%. Raw prefill:
**llama +27-30%** (pp8192 1355 vs 1065 t/s) -- their Ampere GEMM path
is better tuned than my int8 tiles, which were tuned on a 5090, and on
the 5090 the same comparison is parity. Serving-effective prefill
still favors q27 (2309-2569 vs 1720 t/s on real traffic) because of
the prefix cache, but the kernel-for-kernel prefill loss on this
architecture is real and stays in the ledger.

## Running it

```bash
make build/q27-server-w8        # width-8 build; the W12 stack OOMs on 24GB
Q27_KV=turbo3 ./build/q27-server-w8 model.q27 model.tok --port 8080
```

Context auto-sizes to ~131K. The card must be otherwise idle -- 2.7GB
of resident VRAM is the difference between boot and OOM. Use the
default 5.25 bpw model from HF; the heavier quality tiers are
5090-class only (their fixed cost alone exceeds 24GB). One more
tradeoff in the open: a 12-lane engine at width 8 loses ladder
headroom -- that's priced into the 102 t/s number, not hidden under a
5090 config.
