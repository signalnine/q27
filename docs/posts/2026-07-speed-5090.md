On July 5th I filed a measurement protocol: three trials per task, both
engines at their strongest same-day config, same model, same GPU, same
agentic harness, score parity required before any speed claim counts.
On July 10th it ran: **q27 231.3 tokens/sec aggregate vs llama.cpp's
157.4** over 430 and 197 requests -- +47% decode, medians 225 vs 155,
peaks 378 vs 274 -- with task-score medians converged (0.83 == 0.83,
0.78 vs 0.79). The criteria predate the result. That ordering is the
only reason to believe a benchmark from the person who built the
engine.

## What it is

A from-scratch CUDA engine for exactly one model family: Qwen3.6-27B-MTP,
the hybrid GDN/attention model with trained-in MTP draft heads. One
model, one GPU, no abstraction layers. Plain CUDA, no CUTLASS, no
flash-attn. Greedy decode is bitwise-reproducible and gated -- a
canonical 128-token output has held byte-identical through every kernel
rewrite since the gate existed.

## Where the speed comes from

Self-speculation, spent aggressively. The trained MTP head drafts 4-7
deep on an adaptive ladder. A suffix drafter indexes the committed
stream with 4-gram lookup and turns Claude Code's constant re-emission
of its own output into free drafts. Both feed one shared-KV
tensor-core verify, up to 12 lanes wide, arbitrated per round. Live
traffic accepts 5.3-5.8 tokens per weight read -- that IS batching,
pointed at latency instead of cross-user throughput.

Honest scope, sharpened by an external A/B (the maintainer of the
llama.cpp fork my KV quant came from ran his build against mine,
KV-matched): in the *repetition* regime -- full-file re-emission, the
agentic-editing case -- his ngram speculation beats my fused pipeline
653 vs 377 t/s. Its prompt-lookup drafts are unbounded; mine truncate
at a 12-lane verify cap that I've since measured DOES bind here (81% of
suffix fires accept all 12 lanes -- the draft was longer than the cap,
not weaker). Two honest halves to that: against llama's *deployed*
config (draft-mtp, 155 t/s in this regime) my fusion still wins both
regimes, and the ngram mode that beats me is mutually exclusive with
draft-mtp and collapses to 78 t/s on novel prose. The fusion's claim is
robustness -- one good number everywhere without a mode switch -- not a
per-regime crown. Widening the verify cap is the obvious next build.
About 15 points of the 47 are bit-width (my 5.25 bpw repack reads
15.8GB per round vs Q5_K_M's 18.2); the rest is mechanism.

## The structural claim: prefix caching on hybrid-GDN

The recurrent GDN state makes prefix caching all-or-nothing: you cannot
reuse half a recurrent state. vLLM's answer is to disable prefix
caching entirely on this architecture -- measured 0% reuse, every
request re-prefills the whole conversation, 2.5-2.7s per turn at 26K
and growing. q27's answer is state snapshots plus a checkpoint ring:
96.7% reuse over live Claude Code trials, warm turns start in ~50ms.

On real agentic sessions that difference dominates everything else:
vLLM measured 4.7x slower wall-clock on the same tasks. On a same-day
same-payload bench against vLLM's best current config (unsloth's
fixed-MTP NVFP4 checkpoint, which is genuinely the best vLLM leg
anyone has produced for this model -- their MTP export lifts vLLM from
73 to 156 t/s), q27 came out ~2.4x ahead -- and the dominant term in
that ratio is the prefix cache vLLM architecturally cannot run, not
the decode kernels. Stated as a kernel claim it would be misleading;
stated as an architecture claim it is the whole point. sglang 0.5.15
does not load the model at all (GDN weight-loader gaps on both public
quantized checkpoints, as of 2026-07-12).

## Zero config

`./build/q27-server model.q27 model.tok --port 8080` resolves the full
measured stack: fp8 KV, tensor-core verify, ladder + suffix drafter,
context auto-sized to VRAM. It serves a native Anthropic Messages
endpoint at Claude-Code grade -- thinking blocks, tool_use streaming,
count_tokens, context-limit errors shaped so CC compacts correctly.
One env var points Claude Code at it. It binds loopback by default
because it has no auth; exposing it is your explicit call.
